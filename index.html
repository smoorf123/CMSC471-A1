The Rhythm of the City: Temporal Patterns in Chicago Crime

An interactive exploration of how the clock and the calendar dictate urban incidents.

Tulika Kumar & Somil Varshney
CMSC471, March 2026

Cities move to a daily rhythm. There’s the rush of the morning commute, the bustle of lunchtime crowds, and the quiet calm of the late night hours. These patterns shape how people move and behave throughout the day. But does crime follow a similar pattern? To explore this question, we analyzed the Chicago Crimes dataset, a large public dataset collected from the Chicago Police Department’s CLEAR system.

Looking at decades of crime data all at once can blur what’s happening today. To keep our analysis relevant, we focused on the past 12 months. Rather than looking at where crimes happen geographically, we wanted to understand when they happen. By isolating variables like the Primary Type of crime, the Month, the Day of Week, and the Hour, distinct signatures begin to emerge.

Designing for Discovery

To uncover these patterns, we built two interactive visual encodings: a 24x7 Heatmap and a 12-month Line Graph. We avoided geographic maps entirely to focus on spotting overall temporal trends.

The Heatmap uses position (Day vs. Hour) and color intensity (luminance) to encode incident volume, allowing "hotspots" to immediately pop out to the viewer. The accompanying Line Graph uses a shared position scale to track the seasonal ebb and flow of these same incidents over the year.

Static charts can only tell part of the story. To get a deeper look at the data, use the dynamic query selector below and explore it yourself. Try selecting different crime types and see how the patterns change over time. For example, a late-night offense like Battery shows a very different pattern compared to crimes like Theft, which tend to spike during the daytime.

Interpreting the Data

When looking at most crime, specifically in cases like Theft, Narcotics and Burglary, the visualizations reveal two distinct patterns over time: the daily micro-cycle and the yearly macro-cycle.

The Daily Micro-Cycle (Heatmap)

The heatmap highlights a clear pattern in when thefts tend to happen throughout the day. Incidents are at their lowest between 2:00 AM and 6:00 AM, when most people are asleep and businesses are closed, meaning there are fewer opportunities for theft. As the city begins to wake up and daily activity increases, the number of incidents gradually rises. Theft activity reaches its highest levels between 12:00 PM and 7:00 PM, which aligns with the busiest parts of the day. During these hours, more people are out commuting, shopping, and moving through crowded areas, creating more opportunities for things like pickpocketing, retail theft, or unattended belongings being taken.

The Yearly Macro-Cycle (Line Graph)

Zooming out to the 25-year historical trend, three major narratives emerge:

  • The Summer Spike: Crimes like Theft, Battery, Criminal Damage, and Burglary peak every July and August, then plunge to their lowest points in January and February. The Chicago winters act as a natural deterrent, keeping people indoors, while summer lead to open windows, crowded parks, and a rise in reported incidents (see the highlighted regions showing summer months in light yellow making this clearer).
  • The 20-Year Decline: Despite the yearly volatility, the macro trend is clearly downward. The early 2000s regularly saw over 10,000 thefts per month. By recent years, that baseline has dropped by nearly half, hovering between 4,000 and 6,000 (besides Motor Vehicle Theft which shows a very different trend).
  • The 2020 Anomaly: In early 2020, crime data drops sharply as COVID-19 lockdowns changed daily life. With stores closed and far fewer people on the streets, opportunities for crimes like theft and narcotics offenses declined. The sudden dip in the data reflects this brief period when cities were quieter and normal routines were mainly on pause.

Design Decisions

By adding these interactive features, the data becomes more than a dense spreadsheet and turns into a tool for exploration. The dropdown works as a global filter (Category I), allowing users to select a category and instantly update both visualizations with the relevant data.

Furthermore, because aggregated data can sometimes hide the underlying scale, we implemented details-on-demand tooltips (Category II). Hovering over any element in the charts reveals the precise historical incident count, providing context without getting in the way of the initial view.

Methodology & Project Details

Data Sources

  • Dataset: Chicago Crimes, 2001–present (City of Chicago Data Portal). Data was pre-processed using Python (Pandas) to aggregate by hour, day, and month.

Development Process & Work Divided

Our team divided the project based on components of data processing, overall documentation and visual style, and creating the visuals themselves using D3.js to bind the data, scale the axes, etc. We collaborated on the final storytelling and design iteration. This project took around 10 hours to setup, with most of the time taken to process the data.

AI Usage Statement

We utilized AI tools primarily to help write the initial boilerplate for our data aggregation scripts and debugging D3.js issues.
