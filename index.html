The Rhythm of the City: Temporal Patterns in Chicago Crime

An interactive exploration of how the clock and the calendar dictate urban incidents.

Tulika Kumar & Somil Varshney
CMSC471, March 2026

Cities operate on a rhythm. The morning commute, the lunch rush, the quiet of the early hours—all these phases dictate human behavior. But does crime follow a similar, predictable schedule? To answer this, we turned to the Chicago Crimes dataset, an extensive public record extracted from the Chicago Police Department’s CLEAR system.

Because analyzing decades of data at once can obscure recent realities, we focused our lens on the past 12 months. Rather than looking at where crimes happen geographically, we wanted to understand when they happen. By isolating variables like the Primary Type of crime, the Month, the Day of Week, and the Hour, distinct signatures begin to emerge.

Designing for Discovery

To uncover these patterns, we built two interactive visual encodings: a 24x7 Heatmap and a 12-month Line Graph. We avoided geographic maps entirely to focus on spotting overall temporal trends.

The Heatmap uses position (Day vs. Hour) and color intensity (luminance) to encode incident volume, allowing "hotspots" to immediately pop out to the viewer. The accompanying Line Graph uses a shared position scale to track the seasonal ebb and flow of these same incidents over the year.

But static charts only tell part of the story. Use the dynamic query selector below to explore the data yourself. Notice how the temporal footprint of a late-night offense like Battery differs entirely from the daytime spikes of Theft.

Interpreting the Data

When looking at most crime, specifically in cases like Theft, Narcotics and Burglary, the visualizations reveal two distinct temporal realities: the daily micro-cycle and the yearly macro-cycle.

The Daily Micro-Cycle (Heatmap)

The heatmap paints a clear picture of opportunity. Thefts are lowest between 2:00 AM and 6:00 AM, a period when the city is largely asleep and property is secured. However, as the city wakes up, the incident density steadily climbs, reaching its absolute peak between 12:00 PM and 7:00 PM. This perfectly mirrors the rhythm of foot traffic, retail hours, and commuting, which are environments ripe for pickpocketing, retail theft, and unattended belongings.

The Yearly Macro-Cycle (Line Graph)

Zooming out to the 25-year historical trend, three major narratives emerge:

  • The Summer Spike: Crimes like Theft, Battery, Criminal Damage, and Burglary peak every July and August, then plunge to their lowest points in January and February. The Chicago winters act as a natural deterrent, keeping people indoors, while summer brings open windows, crowded parks, and a surge in reported incidents (see the highlighted regions showing summer months in light yellow making this clearer).
  • The 20-Year Decline: Despite the yearly volatility, the macro trend is undeniably downward. The early 2000s regularly saw over 10,000 thefts per month. By recent years, that baseline has dropped by nearly half, hovering between 4,000 and 6,000 (besides Motor Vehicle Theft which shows a completely different story).
  • The 2020 Anomaly: We see a sharp cratering of the data in early 2020 for crimes like Theft and Narcotics. As COVID-19 lockdowns emptied the streets and closed retail stores, these crimes dropped significantly.

Design Decisions

By implementing these interactive techniques, the data transforms from a dense spreadsheet into an exploratory tool. The dropdown acts as a global filter (Category I), dynamically re-binding data to both visualizations simultaneously.

Furthermore, because aggregated data can sometimes hide the underlying scale, we implemented details-on-demand tooltips (Category II). Hovering over any element in the charts reveals the precise historical incident count, providing context without cluttering the initial view.

Methodology & Project Details

Data Sources

  • Dataset: Chicago Crimes, 2001–present (City of Chicago Data Portal). Data was pre-processed using Python (Pandas) to aggregate by hour, day, and month.

Development Process & Work Split

Our team divided the project based on data processing, overall documentation and visual style, and creating the visuals themselves using D3.js to bind the data, scale the axes, etc. We collaborated on the final storytelling and design iteration. This project took roughly 10 hours to setup, with most of the time taken to process the data.

AI Usage Statement

We utilized AI tools primarily to help write the initial boilerplate for our data aggregation scripts and debugging D3.js issues.
